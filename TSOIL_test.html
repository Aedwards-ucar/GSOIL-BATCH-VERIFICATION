⚠ Preliminary Analysis — Known Limitations: This is an exploratory calculation from a first-pass calibration test with several uncharacterized variables. (1) Volumetric heat capacity Cv = 1.2 MJ m⁻³ K⁻¹ is assumed for dry sand — actual sandbox sand properties (bulk density, moisture, organic content) were not measured during this test and will affect the storage estimate. (2) The 4 Tsoil probes are positioned between the Gsoil sensor clusters (see layout diagram), not co-located with individual plates, so spatial averaging is approximate. (3) Gsoil plates in dry sand are known to underestimate true flux by 2–38% due to thermal contact resistance and heat flow distortion (Sauer et al., 2003 [6]). (4) The lamp-off transient produces large dT/dt spikes that may briefly saturate the storage calculation. A future test with measured soil thermal properties (TP01 or similar) will substantially improve these estimates.

Method — Combination (Plate + Calorimetric) Approach:

The surface heat flux G(0) cannot be measured directly because heat flux plates must be buried below the surface to avoid disturbing the near-surface thermal regime. The standard approach, established by Fuchs & Tanner (1968) [1] and described clearly in Cobos & Baker (2003) [2], estimates G(0) by correcting the plate reading for heat stored in the soil layer above the plate:

G(0) = G(zp) + ΔS

where G(zp) is the Gsoil plate flux at depth zp = 0.05 m, and the storage term ΔS is computed by the calorimetric method [1,2,3]:

ΔS = Cv · (ΔT̄ / Δt) · zp

T̄ is the arithmetic mean of the 4 Tsoil temperatures spanning 0.6–4.4 cm depth, taken as representative of the mean temperature of the 0–5 cm layer. Δt = 600 s (10-min interval). Cv = 1.2 × 10⁶ J m⁻³ K⁻¹ is the assumed volumetric heat capacity of dry sand [4]. This value is within the range reported by Sauer et al. (2003) [5] for dry sand (1.09–1.48 MJ m⁻³ K⁻¹) but should be verified with in-situ measurement for a future test.

Ochsner et al. (2007) [3] demonstrated that omitting the storage correction can cause plates at ~6 cm depth to underestimate G(0) by more than 50% — consistent with what we observe here, where ΔS accounts for approximately —% of G(0) at peak. Gao et al. (2017) [7] confirm the calorimetric method as the reference standard for G(0) estimation.
